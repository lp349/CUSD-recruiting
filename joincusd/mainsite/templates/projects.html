Past Projects

Sustainable Neighborhoods Nicaragua

Sustainable Neighborhoods Nicaragua (SNN) worked to change the face of housing in the country by designing and building a healthy, sustainable, and low-cost home model in Nagarote, Nicaragua. Based in San Diego, Nicaragua, and grown out of the need to find solutions to address Nicaragua’s housing shortage problem, the SNN project utilized community-based design, low-tech building technologies, local materials, education, and capacity building to create a replicable model for future low-income housing developments in Nicaragua.

Since our development of the model home, the Nicaraguan government has heralded our design as a practical, innovative, and applicable solution to the nation’s housing issues. Today, the nonprofit Sostenica continues to construct our designs throughout the country.

Sustainable Research Facility

This long-term project aimed to design a first-of-its-kind facility for interdisciplinary sustainability research on Cornell’s Ithaca Campus. The project brought together 150 students, faculty members, researchers, and organizations from across eleven different departments at Cornell in an unprecedented interdisciplinary effort, establishing the connections and relationships that led to CUSD’s involvement on the Cornell Tech project, the solar energy study of the Ithaca campus, and the Upson and Rice Hall renovation projects. The SRF project also set the standard on campus for the cross-college collaboration and engaged learning that is today such a central aspect of Cornell’s academic mission.

Schoolhouse South Africa

Schoolhouse South Africa was a two-year project to design and construct a 6,000 square foot early childhood development center and teacher training facility in Cosmo City, South Africa. Grown out of collaboration with local groups and residents, the schoolhouse is a landmark and source of pride for the developing community. With classes held throughout all hours of the day, SSA is a beacon for education and passive sustainable technologies both in the local community and around the world. The building leverages low-maintenance, sustainable, passive heating and cooling technologies, and includes four classrooms, a dining area, kitchen, indoor and outdoor play areas, and an office. Interactive spaces provide sanctuaries for group learning, creative play, and social development.

Developed over the course of two years, the project consisted of two semesters of scoping and research, a semester of design development in coordination with the second year architecture comprehensive design studio, and three months of construction. Students collaborated with local partners and stakeholders to develop, refine, promote, and construct the schoolhouse. The project has been recognized by Architizer, Engineering News Record, and Global Best Project Awards.
